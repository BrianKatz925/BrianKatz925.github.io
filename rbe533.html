RBE533: Smart Materials

Summary

In RBE533, as part of a team of five, I helped develop a robot that could overcome a series of obstacles in series to prove the effectiveness of smart materials. The motivation behind this project was the field of search and rescue, and how the usage of smart materials can drastically improve the capabilities of locomotion and actuation. In the final project, our robot was able to survive a fall, drive over a slippery ramp, climb stairs, and swim through water.

The robot featured a tank-tread style direct drive to increase traction to the ground on inclined surfaces, and a pair of paddles that could push the robot above stairs and through water. We also added a shape memory-alloy buffer to act as a shock absorber during the fall along with an inflatable balloon to increase buoyancy in water.

To learn more about our project in detail, view our final report here. and our presentation here..

Mechanical Design

For this robot, we had to think critically about our material choices, since each challenge was unique. To overcome the fall, our robot needed to absorb the shock while being lightweight such that it was buoyant in water. We decided to use a low-density 3D print for the chassis to minimize print time and weight. Through testing, we found additional weight was required in the rear to increase traction, as the robot's center of gravity was towards the front. We also added sealing components on the top lid using 1.75mm 85A TPU as a peripheral seal and silicone adhesive on all other ingress points.

We moved the shock-absorption capabilities to our paddles which we 3D printed from Shore Hardness 95A TPU, an excellent shock absorption and high friction material. To drive the paddles, we used a high-torque brushless DC motor with an external 1:3 gear ratio and fused one of the 3D printed gears directly to the paddle with heat. The paddles were incredibly effective, pushing the robot over individual steps and propelling the robot forward in water.

Mechanical Design

As our mode of locomotion, we used a belt-driven tank drive as it would provide us with increased surface area over a traditional wheeled robot. We designed the belts to feature a pulley v-profile with a chevron outer pattern for increased friction. The entire belt was 3D printed in two pieces from 85A TPU and fused together with epoxy. This belt was excellent in maintaining traction, especially on the low-friction incline, as the high contact region and power of the motors overcame the low friction.

Our last main feature was a shock absorption mechanism to aid with the drop challenge. Using shape memory alloys, we designed a compliant mechanism that would retract upon the application of current to the SMA coils. The SMAs we selected were programmed by wrapping them around the threads of a screw, heating them to 550F and quenching them in water, forming a set of linear actuators.

Electronics and Software

We used two ESP32 Development Kits, communicating over ESP-NOW WiFi. One ESP was mounted to a handheld controller which we designed with breadboard buttons and video game joysticks, while the other was inside the robot chassis, receiving commands and controlling the motors. To power the high-torque motors, we connected an external power supply, supplying 14V directly to two dual-motor drivers, supporting up to 3A each.
